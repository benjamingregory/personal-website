Open Unmap and you get a black map of San Francisco. Not a stylized dark theme; actually dark, like
the lights are off. There are no labels. Nothing is named. You can still tell it's your city, because
the streets and the shoreline and the parks read through the gloom as faint geometry, but the map
refuses to tell you what any of it is called.

Then you reveal a place you've been, and the building it sits in lights up. Not a dot, not a glowing
circle. The actual footprint of that one building, warm against the dark. The pitch is *your city, in
the dark*, and you earn it back one address at a time.

From the outside that sounds like dots on a map. Put points on a basemap, light the ones the user has
visited, done. I believed that for about a day. This post is about the three places it turned out not
to be that, and the wrong turns I took in each one.

## The fog had to be navigable and anonymous at the same time

The first version was a near-total blackout: a fog layer at `rgba(3,5,12,0.985)` painted over the
whole map. It looked great in a screenshot and was useless to actually use. You couldn't orient. With
the city that dark and the labels gone, you were staring into a void with a few glowing dots in it,
and you had no idea whether the dot you cared about was in the Mission or the Marina.

That forced the actual product decision, which I should have made on purpose instead of backing into:
the city should be **navigable but anonymous**. You can see where you are. You cannot see what
anything is called. Names fill in only as you explore.

Those are two different jobs, and they're handled in two different places:

- **Navigable** is the fog. I dropped `FOG_COLOR` from that near-opaque value to `rgba(3,5,12,0.45)`,
  so it reads as a dim tint instead of a blackout. Streets, water, and parks show through as
  silhouette. You get orientation without legibility.
- **Anonymous** is the labels. Every `symbol` layer in the basemap style is set to
  `visibility: none`. The geometry stays (that's the navigable part); the text goes (that's the
  anonymous part). I also softened the corner vignette so it frames the view rather than carving a
  dark circle out of it.

The lesson I keep having to relearn: "make it look cool" and "make it usable" are not the same slider.
The blackout was the cool version. Splitting cool into *navigable* plus *anonymous* was the usable
one.

## Light the building, not a blob

A reveal should light the building at that address. That's the whole emotional payload of the product,
so a fuzzy circle wasn't going to cut it. This is the part that went through the most wrong turns, and
they're worth writing down because each one looked correct until it didn't.

**Wrong turn one: a glowing disc.** The first reveal was a 240-meter radial glow centered on the
point. Too blobby. It lit a neighborhood, not a building, and it killed the entire "that's *my*
building" feeling.

**Wrong turn two: trace the building geometry from the basemap.** Reasonable next move: the basemap
already has `building` features, so query the one under the point and outline it. Except the basemap's
`building` features are **MultiPolygons that bundle many buildings per tile**. One feature I pulled
had roughly 180 separate polygons in it. Tracing the feature lit the entire block. The fix was to pull
out the *single* polygon that actually contains the venue point (a point-in-polygon test), or the
nearest building-sized one within a snap radius if the point sat just off its footprint.

**Wrong turn three, sort of: defending against merged geometry.** At low zoom the basemap hands back
coarse, merged shapes, so I added a `MAX_FOOTPRINT_M` guard (250 meters) that rejects anything bigger
than a plausible building and falls back to a soft glow. Then I added a refine-on-zoom pass that
re-queries as the finer building tiles stream in and swaps a coarse polygon for a tighter one, never
the other way around.

All of that worked. It was also the wrong architecture, and I only saw why once it was running. Two
problems wouldn't go away. The footprints **took a beat to appear**, because they only resolved once
you'd zoomed in far enough for the building tiles to load. And in dense areas the basemap merges
attached buildings into one block polygon, so there simply was no per-address geometry to find, no
matter how clever the query.

Both problems have the same root cause: I was deriving geometry on the client, at runtime, from a
source that didn't have it at the resolution I needed. So I stopped doing that and computed the
footprints **once, server-side**, from real data.

The source is [DataSF's Building Footprints dataset](https://data.sfgov.org/Geographic-Locations-and-Boundaries/Building-Footprints/ynuv-fyni),
roughly 177,000 individual building polygons, authoritative and per-building, no GDAL or DuckDB
gymnastics required. An ingest script loads all of them into a scratch PostGIS table, and for each
venue finds the building it's contained by (or the nearest within about 40 meters), then stores a
simplified footprint right on the venue row. Coverage came out to **12,441 of 12,680 venues, about
98%**.

One detail mattered more than I expected: the simplify tolerance. At 2 meters, PostGIS flattened
detailed buildings into quads, around 9 vertices each, and the city started looking like it was made
of shoeboxes. Dropping to 0.4 meters kept the L-shapes and notches that make a building look like
itself (about 18 vertices each), at the cost of growing the payload from 5.5 MB to 8.4 MB. Worth every
byte.

The client now reads the footprint straight off each venue. The entire runtime stack I'd been so proud
of (the tile query, the point-in-polygon test, the size guards, the refine-on-zoom) got deleted.
Footprints are now instant at any zoom, including the moment the map first loads, which is exactly when
you want them.

Oh, and I prototyped 3D extruded buildings and threw them away. The flat footprint, the blueprint look,
was the right call, and the basemap has no usable per-building height across most of the city anyway.

## The bug that hid most of the city

This is the one that cost me the most time, and the one I'd most like to save you from.

The original data loading re-queried a bounding-box function on every `moveend` and *replaced* the
whole venue set with the result. So panning the map waited on a network round trip, and dots blinked
out and re-popped as you dragged. Worse, a zoomed-out viewport held more venues than the query's row
cap, so the returned subset reshuffled every time you moved. The map felt like it was made of jelly.

The fix is almost embarrassingly blunt: load the whole city once and never query on pan again. San
Francisco is only about **12,680 venues**, roughly 250 KB gzipped. That's nowhere near the ~50,000-point
range where the MapLibre and deck.gl guidance says you should start reaching for vector tiles. Vector
tiles are the right answer at scale and complete overkill here. So: one request, cache it, render
everything.

I shipped that, reloaded, and only downtown rendered. The rest of the city was empty.

The cause took a while to find because it was invisible from where I was looking. PostgREST, which sits
under Supabase's REST layer, enforces a `db-max-rows` limit, and on Supabase that default is **1,000
rows**. When a query returns more, PostgREST doesn't error. It silently returns the first 1,000 by scan
order and moves on. My venues happened to cluster downtown by insert order, so the first thousand rows
were all downtown, and the city beyond it just didn't exist as far as the client knew. The same cap had
quietly been truncating the old bounding-box query too; I'd never caught it because my `psql` tests ran
against the database directly and bypassed PostgREST entirely. The tool I was using to verify the data
was the one tool that couldn't see the bug.

The fix is a nice little PostgREST trick. The row cap applies to row *sets*. So I rewrote the function
to return a single `jsonb` array instead of a set of rows. A scalar result is one row by definition, the
cap doesn't apply, and all 12,680 venues come back in one response with their footprints embedded.
`supabase-js` hands the array straight back as data.

Caching went into IndexedDB rather than localStorage, because with footprints embedded the payload is
about 8.4 MB and localStorage caps out around 5. It's cache-first on load (a fresh hit renders the
whole city with zero network), a 7-day TTL, and a version prefix in the cache key so that older,
footprint-less, or truncated caches invalidate themselves automatically when the shape changes.

## One more, because it's a good trap

A smaller one worth flagging if you're on a recent React. The dots are drawn by deck.gl, mounted as an
interleaved overlay so deck renders into MapLibre's own WebGL context instead of a second canvas
stacked on top. One GL context, no manual per-frame camera syncing, the dots ride the map's render
loop.

The documented way to feed it new props is to call `setProps` in the render body. Under Next.js 16 and
its React Compiler, that throws "Rules of Hooks" errors at runtime. Moving the `setProps` call into a
`useEffect` fixes it cleanly. I verified it the boring way: the separate-canvas version was clean,
in-render `setProps` errored, effect-based `setProps` was clean. Sometimes the documented pattern and
your framework's newest rules just disagree, and you find out at runtime.

## The point

Three things turned "dots on a map" into something that actually feels like fog of war over a real
city, and none of them was the part I expected to be hard.

First, **a scalar result dodges a row cap your direct database tests can't see.** PostgREST capping a
row set at 1,000 and returning the first thousand by scan order is the kind of bug that hides in plain
sight, especially when your verification path bypasses the exact layer doing the truncating. Returning
one `jsonb` array made the cap irrelevant.

Second, **precomputing geometry once, server-side, beats any amount of clever client-side derivation.**
The runtime tile-query stack was genuinely clever and it was the wrong call. A spatial join against a
real buildings dataset gave me per-address footprints that are instant at every zoom, and let me delete
hundreds of lines of guards and refinement logic.

Third, **the product decision was the engineering decision.** "Navigable but anonymous" wasn't a tagline
I bolted on at the end. It's why the fog is a tint and not a blackout, why every label layer is off, and
why a reveal lights one building's exact footprint instead of a glowing blob. The constraints made the
feeling, and the feeling was the whole point.
